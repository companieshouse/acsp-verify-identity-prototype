Statutory guidance

Companies House identity verification standard

Companies House identity verification standard outlines what authorised agents must do to verify someone’s identity to the level required by the Companies Act.

1. Get evidence of the claimed identity

You’ll need to know a combination of information about the person whose identity you are checking. This is known as their claimed identity.

As a minimum, you must know their:

  • name
  • date of birth
  • home address
  • nationality

The person will need to show you identity documents to prove this information so you can verify their identity.

Documents you can use as evidence

Depending on what documents the person can provide, there are 3 routes to verify someone’s identity.

If the person is a resident outside of the UK, the documents they provide must include a government issued photographic identity document.

Route 1

The person must provide one document from group A.

You must use this route if the person can provide the document.

Route 2

You can only use this if the person cannot provide a document from group A.

The person must show you 2 different documents in total. It must include:

  • one document from group B
  • a different document from group B, C or D

Route 3

You can only use this if the person is a resident of the UK and does not have documents from groups A or B.

The person must be able to show 3 different documents in total. It must include:

  • one document from group C
  • a different document from group D
  • a different document from group E

You’ll also need to take a photograph of the person. If you cannot take a photograph, you must ask for a photograph and do a likeness check.

We are more likely to contact you for more information if you have checked someone’s identity using this route. It provides a lower level of assurance, so you may need to verify the person’s identity again.

Valid identity documents

Documents must only be included once in the document count. For example, 2 banks statements from the same bank will not count as 2 separate documents.

2. Check the evidence is genuine and valid

If you want to prove someone’s identity using information that’s based on physical evidence, you must check it is genuine. This means that the evidence is unlikely to be forged or counterfeit.

You must check that documents:

  • are valid – this means you can find records that show the document has been issued
  • have not been reported as lost or stolen
  • have not expired
  • are in the person’s name
  • confirm the person’s date of birth and home address

You should always check identity documents for signs of tampering. You must query if there are any signs of damage to a document. This is particularly important if the damage is in an area where personal details are shown, such as the name or photograph.

Read the National Document Fraud Unit (NDFU) guidance on examining identity documents for more information on how you can detect basic forgeries.

Checking digital documents

This includes printed versions of digital documents.

Digital documents often provide a lower level of assurance than physical documents. This is because physical documents that have been posted to a person’s address prove that the person has access to that address.

You can accept printed versions of digital documents, but you should consider the lower level of assurance when accepting these as evidence.

Checking documents issued by a country outside of the UK

If you are concerned a document form outside the UK may not be genuine, you should check it by either:

  • contacting the relevant embassy or consulate
  • using the Public Register of Authentic identity and travel Documents Online (PRADO)

If documents are in a foreign language, you must check they provide evidence of the customer’s identity. The Registrar may require certified translations when inspecting your identity verification records.

3. Check the claimed identity has existed over time

This is also known as an ‘active history’ check. It lowers the risk of you accepting:

  • a synthetic identity
  • an identity that belongs to someone who has died

You should be aware that some parts of a person’s identity may change over time, for example their name or gender. This can be for legitimate reasons so is not always a sign of higher risk.

Check their address history

The person will need to give you their address history for the last 5 years. Where possible, you should ask to see documentation to confirm the address history.

Check if they have changed their name

You must ask the person about any previous name changes. You should ask them to show you documentation to support any changes of name.

If they cannot show you proof of the name change, you should find out why and consider whether to verify their identity.

You can usually accept documents which show the person’s name as a synonym. For example, a document might say the person’s name is Samantha on their passport, but Sam on their bank card.

Check if they have regularly interacted with other organisations or people

You can check the person’s interactions over time by asking to see evidence of their:

  • credit card activity
  • gas or electric account payments
  • online bank or retail account activity
  • travel to another country
  • health records
  • employment records, for example payslips
  • school records

The person will need to give you their address history for the last 5 years. Where possible, you should ask to see documentation to confirm the address history.

4. Check if the claimed identity is at high risk of fraud

You must keep records to show you have attempted to make sure the person’s identity is not:

  • at a higher risk of identity fraud than usual
  • a synthetic identity

To do this, you can check the details of the claimed identity with counter-fraud data sources, such as a national fraud database.

You should make sure the person’s identity has not been stolen or reported as stolen. You can also use counter-fraud data sources to check if the identity:

  • belongs to someone who is alive
  • is known by an organisation that should have a record of that person (for example an Electoral Registration Office in a local authority)
  • is at a higher risk of being impersonated, for example if they are a politically exposed person like a politician or judge

5. Check that the identity belongs to the person who’s claiming it

If possible, you should ask for biometric or photographic identity documents (or both). This is so you can compare the document with the person to check that:

  • the person physically matches the photo on (or related to) the document
  • the person matches the biometric information shown on the document or a trusted data source

Read more about how you can make sure someone matches biometric information in the government guidance on proving and verifying someone’s identity.

You will need to check the:

  • documents are real
  • person is a real person – this is also known as a ‘liveness’ check
  • person is the same person as in the document photograph (also known as a ‘likeness’ check)

6. Tell Companies House you have verified someone’s identity

When you have completed checks to verify someone’s identity, you will need to send a verification statement to the registrar. To do this, you need to Tell Companies House you have verified someone’s identity.

You needs to tell us the:

  • person’s name
  • person’s date of birth
  • person’s home address
  • date you completed the identity checks
  • documents you used as evidence to verify their identity

You’ll also need to complete a declaration to confirm that the person is who they claim to be.

If your checks meet the Companies House standard of identity verification, we will send the person their Companies House personal code.

They will need this to prove their verified status to (do any of the following):

  • file with Companies House
  • be a director
  • be a Person of Significant Control (PSC)

Other ways to verify someone’s identity

You can use an identity platform or commercial provider to verify someone’s identity.

If you do this, you must:

  • ensure the checks are carried out correctly and meet Companies House identity verification standard
  • keep copies of documents or other evidence

What to do if you cannot verify someone’s identity

The person will need to get documents to be able to verify their identity for Companies House.

If you cannot file annual accounts because you need more time to verify someone’s identity, you can apply to extend the accounts filing deadline.

You must do this before the normal filing deadline.

Further guidance on checking identity documents

Checking a driving licence

English, Welsh and Scottish driving licence numbers contain information about the applicant’s name, sex, and date of birth. This information is written in a special format but can be gleaned and matched against the information provided by the applicant.

Please note that the date of birth on English, Welsh, and Scottish driving licences, issued before 1977, is not recorded as a separate entry on the licence. The date of birth can be deciphered from the driving licence number and checked against the date of birth field on the application form.

For example, the format of the number for Christine Josephine Robinson, born 2 July 1975:

R O B I N 7 5 7 0 2 5 C J 9 9 9 0 1

N N N N N Y M M D D Y I I C C C C C

N = 1st five letters of the surname (if the surname begins MAC or MC it is treated as MC for all)

Y = Year of birth

M = Month of birth (In the case of a female, the number represented by the first M will have the value 5 added to the first digit, for example, a female born in November (i.e. 11) would display ‘61’ in the MM boxes or if born in February (i.e. 02) would display ‘52’

D = Day of month of birth

I = Initial letter of the first two forenames - if only one, then 9 will replace the second letter; if the licence indicates that the applicant has a middle name, ensure that one has been provided in section A

C = Computer generated

For Northern Ireland, Isle of Man, and Jersey driving licences the licence number is in a different format. The licence number is unique to the driver and the ‘name’ or ‘date of birth’ validation, as shown above, cannot be used.

Checking a photo driving licence

Examine the licence for evidence of photo tampering or any amendment of the printed details.

Checking an old style driving licence (no photograph)

Remove the document from the plastic wallet and check that it is printed on both sides.

It should have a watermark visible by holding the licence up to the light and there should be no punctuation marks in the name or address.

The ‘Valid To’ date should be the day before the bearer’s 70th birthday (unless the bearer is already over 70). The ‘Valid To’ date can therefore be cross-referenced with the applicant’s date of birth detailed in section A.

Checking a passport

Check the general quality and condition of the passport. Treat it with suspicion if it is excessively damaged; accidental damage is often used to conceal tampering.

Photographs should be examined closely for signs of damage to the laminate or for excessive glue or slitting of the laminate; these signs would indicate photo substitution. If the photograph appears excessively large, this might indicate an attempt to hide another photograph underneath. There should also be an embossed strip embedded into the laminate, which will catch a portion of the photograph.

Check there is no damage to this area. If the passport is from a foreign national, you can still follow the same procedures as above.

HM Passport Office has produced a guide to be used when checking passports for identification.

Checking a birth certificate

Birth certificates are not evidence of identity, and are easily obtained. Although certificates issued at the time of birth may give more confidence that it belongs to the individual, unlike a recently issued certificate, they will not show if any information has been corrected or superseded by a new registration.

Check the quality of paper used; genuine certificates use a high grade. There should be a watermark visible when the document is held up to the light. Any signs of smoothness on the surface would indicate that original text might have been washed or rubbed away. There should be no signs of tampering, changes using liquid paper, overwriting or spelling mistakes.

The following list provides some general information about certificate completion which may help to establish whether the certificate and/or the details have been falsified. This is provided solely as a guide and is not exhaustive:

  • The certificate format used should be appropriate for the year of registration
  • Only the surname should be entered in upper case, not the forename(s)
  • Dates of birth should be shown with the day and month in words and the year in figures

The following information might indicate that the certificate has been altered:

  • Spacing between falsely added particulars might be irregular compared to original information. ‘Thick’ or ‘thin’ spacing might infer particulars have been added
  • False particulars might not have been aligned with other words
  • Characters may not be of the same size or shape with the rest of the particulars
  • Movement of handwriting may look mechanical and does not flow with the rest of the particulars
  • Changes might not be consistent e.g. parents’ surnames might be altered, but not the signatures
  • The area around falsely added or removed particulars may react differently under an ultra violet light i.e. show signs of staining. In addition, such areas of paper may appear thinner where the paper fibres have been disturbed by abrasion

For more information on checking birth certificates, please refer to HM Passport Office document General Register Office guide to birth certificates.

Checking an EEA photo identity card

Examine the card for evidence of photo tampering or any amendment of the printed details.

Checking an HM Forces ID card

Examine the card for evidence of photo tampering or any amendment of the printed details.

Checking a firearms licence

Check the licence is printed on blue security paper with a Royal crest watermark and a faint pattern stating the words ‘Home Office’.

Examine the licence for evidence of photo tampering or any amendment of the printed details, which should include home address and date of birth.

The licence should be signed by the holder and bear the authorising signature of the chief of police for the area in which they live, or normally a person to whom his authority has been delegated.

Checking online evidence of immigration status

Ensure that evidence is always taken from a .gov.uk web address.

Further information on how to view evidence of immigration and right to work status can be found here: View a job applicant’s right to work details - GOV.UK

Checking a biometric residence permit

View the features of a permit and how to check a job applicant’s biometric residence permit to see if they have a right to work in the UK.

Other types of ID

Ensure all letters and statements are recent, i.e. within a three month period. Do not accept documentation printed from the internet.

Check letter headed paper is used, bank headers are correct and all documentation looks genuine. The address should be cross-referenced with that provided by the applicant.
